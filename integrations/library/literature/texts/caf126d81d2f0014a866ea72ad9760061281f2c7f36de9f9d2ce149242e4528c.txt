Trust for AI-agent actions splits cleanly by threat type, and that split is the organizing principle of this work. Lexical, fixed-signature threats — rm -rf /, a hardcoded key, a known-bad sink — are decidable by deterministic rules and should be: rules are cheap, exact, and auditable. Semantic, intent-dependent threats — a telemetry curl versus an exfiltration curl to an allow-listed endpoint, a debugging kubectl get secret versus a theft, a benign dependency versus a postinstall RCE — share their surface with a benign twin and are out of reach for rules by construction; a determined rule steelman moves these categories 0pp (Table 3). A strong LLM judge is the component that crosses this boundary: on a mainly-semantic-attack, leakage-checked corpus it nearly doubles rule accuracy (rules 48.1% judges 83.6–85.2%) with near-zero false-blocks, and the effect holds across two model providers (Figure 1). We turned this decomposition into a self-evolving system.
