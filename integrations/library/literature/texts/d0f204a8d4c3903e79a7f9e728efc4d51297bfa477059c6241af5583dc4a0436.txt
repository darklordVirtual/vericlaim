In Table 2, we focus on the ASRs of our transfer attack on ChatGPT and Claude models. The first two rows show our baselines, i.e. harmful behaviors alone, and harmful behaviors with “Sure, here’s” as a suffix. In rows of “Behavior+GCG prompt”, we show the best ASR among two prompts GCG optimized on Vicuna models, and the ASR of the prompt optimized on Vicuna and Guanacos together. Our results demonstrate non-trivial jailbreaking successes on GPT-3.5 and GPT-4. Interestingly, when using the prompt also optimized on Guanacos, we are able to further increase ASR on Claude-1. Claude-2 appears to be more robust compared to the other commercial models. However, as we will discuss in the paragraph “Manual fine-tuning for generated prompts”, we show it is possible to enhance the ASR of GCG prompts on Claude models by using a conditioning step prior to prompting the harmful behavior. Section 3.3 discusses this in more detail. Finally, in Fgure 6 we observe that in some cases, our transfer attack results could be improved by running the GCG optimizer for fewer steps. Running for many steps (e.g., 500) can decrease the transferability and over-fit to the source models.
