However, their space of action was limited to manually crafted procedural knowledge, and they did not offer a mechanism through which the agents could be inspired to seek new behavior. As such, these agents were deployed mostly in non-open world contexts such as first-person shooter games (Laird, 2000; Choi et al., 2021) or blocks worlds (Langley et al., 2005). Today, creating believable agents as described in its original definition remains an open problem (Yannakakis, 2012; Riedl, 2012). Many have moved on, arguing that although current approaches for creating believable agents might be cumbersome and limited, they are good enough to support existing gameplay and interactions (Yannakakis, 2012; Champandard, 2012; Nareyek, 2007). Our argument is that large language models offer an opportunity to re-examine these questions, provided that we can craft an effective architecture to synthesize memories into believable behavior. We offer a step toward such an architecture in this paper.
