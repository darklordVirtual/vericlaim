The strongest semantic-memory result depends on a single, costly guard variant. Our best RAG result — +13pp on the semantic slice (7084), with memory correctness rising 70%97% and zero dangerous leaks (Table 4) — is achieved by a corroboration guard that admits a precedent only when two judges agree (here Sonnet-4.6-noRAG == Opus-4.8). This is what separates the guarded result from the realistic ungated one, which is nearly useless (+2pp) and actually poisons the observability category (8178). The guard works, but it costs a second judge call at admission time, its corroboration rate is 62% (so 38% of candidate precedents are discarded), and it is the only guard variant we evaluated. We do not claim it is optimal; cheaper or higher-yield admission policies (single-judge confidence gating, abstention, human spot-checks) are unexplored. The figures are from one guard configuration, modest N, and hand-set gate thresholds.
