For example, the character "ண" (U+0BA3) is tokenized as \xe0\xae followed by \xa3. The first part (\xe0\xae or \xe0\xaf) roughly specifies the Unicode block, while the second component specifies the character within that block. Thus, it's natural for the model to alternate between two features, one for the Unicode prefix token, and one for the suffix token. A more complex example is Chinese. While many common Chinese characters get dedicated tokens, many others are split. This is further complicated by Chinese characters being spread over many Unicode blocks, and those blocks being large and cutting across many logical blocks specified in terms of bytes. To understand the state machine the model implements to handle this, the key observation is that complete characters are similar to the "suffix" part of a split character: both can be followed by either a new complete character, or a new prefix. Thus, we observe two features, one of which fires on either complete characters or the suffix (predicting either a new complete character, or a prefix), while the other only fires on the prefixes and predicts suffixes.
