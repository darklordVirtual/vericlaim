Historically, these agents were developed in the context of intelligent game non-player characters (NPCs) (Laird and VanLent, 2001; Riedl, 2012). Creating NPCs with believable behavior, if possible, could enhance player experiences in games and interactive fictions by enabling emergent narratives (Swartout et al., 2006; Aylett, 1999; Brenner, 2010; Ibister and Nass, 2000) and social interactions with the agents (Zubek, 2002). However, more importantly, game worlds provide increasingly realistic representations of real-world affordances, and as observed by Laird and van Lent in 2001, these simulated worlds offer accessible testbeds for developers of believable agents to finesse the agents’ cognitive capabilities without worrying about implementing robotics in the real world or creating simulation environments from scratch (Laird and VanLent, 2001; Riedl, 2012). A diverse set of approaches to creating believable agents emerged over the past four decades. In implementation, however, these approaches often simplified the environment or dimensions of agent behavior to make the effort more manageable (Brooks et al., 2000; Minsky and Papert, 1970).
