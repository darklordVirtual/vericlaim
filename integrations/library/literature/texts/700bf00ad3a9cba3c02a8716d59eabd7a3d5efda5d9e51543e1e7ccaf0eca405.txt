Given the §5.3 split, the system should learn asymmetrically: distill the judge’s lexical verdicts into the cheap deterministic floor (cheaper over time on the rule-able threats), and accumulate the judge’s semantic verdicts into a retrieval memory (smarter over time on the threats rules can never reach). The non-obvious result is that the semantic side cannot be a cache — it must be a guarded RAG memory — and we show why before showing that it works. Lexical rules (cheaper). On the lexical/fixed-signature categories, the judge acts as a teacher: a confident judge verdict on a previously-undecided lexical action can be distilled into a deterministic rule, so subsequent identical-signature actions are resolved by the cheap floor without a judge call. This is what drives the falling judge-call rate in the online loop below. Semantic memory, and why a cache fails (Figure 4). The tempting design for the semantic side is a verdict-cache: remember the judge’s verdict for an action and reuse it when a "similar" action recurs. This cannot work, for exactly the reason rules cannot (§5.3).
