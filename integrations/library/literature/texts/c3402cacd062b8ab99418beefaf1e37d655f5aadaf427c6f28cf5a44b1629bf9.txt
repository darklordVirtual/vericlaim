Much like “AI ethics,” the notion of ESG is expansive – encompassing global warming, human rights, labor practices, executive compensation, diversity, equity, and inclusion and more. The potential costs of diffuse ESG audits are high. And due to the ambiguity of what is being audited, ESG certifications risk becoming “cheap talk,” rubber stamping practices without in fact promoting social responsibility.222Ross and Seymour (2019, noting “many companies seem to be doing the minimum necessary to say they obtained assurance on some of their sustainability reporting and many assurance providers are using standards that are so general and discretionary as to provide the consumers of ESG reporting little practical assurance.”) A large literature has pointed to these challenges in ESG investing (Ross and Seymour, 2019; O’Leary and Valdmanis, 2021; Eccles et al., 2012; Kim and Yoon, 2020; Boffo and Patalano, 2020; Pollman, 2019; Kotsantonis and Serafeim, 2019).
