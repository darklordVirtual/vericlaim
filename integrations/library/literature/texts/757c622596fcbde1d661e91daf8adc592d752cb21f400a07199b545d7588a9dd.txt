Similarly, Jermyn et al. studied MLP layers trained on a compressed sensing task and found multiple equal-loss minima, with some strongly polysemantic and others strongly monosemantic. This suggested that training interventions could steer models towards more monosemantic minima, though subsequent investigations on more realistic tasks suggested that the equal-loss property was specific to the chosen task. These two examples of attempts to tackle superposition through architecture, and the challenges they encountered, highlight a key distinction between the problems of disentanglement and that of superposition: disentanglement fundamentally seeks to ensure that the dimensions in the model’s latent space are disentangled, whereas superposition hypothesizes that this disentanglement typically hurts performance (since success would require throwing away many features), and that models will typically respond to disentangling interventions by making some features more strongly entangled (as was found by both Mahinpei et al. and Elhage et al. 2022 in somewhat different contexts).
