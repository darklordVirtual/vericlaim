Prior literature has described believability, or believable agents, as a central design and engineering goal. Believable agents are designed to provide an illusion of life and present a facade of realism in the way they appear to make decisions and act on their own volition, similar to the characters in Disney movies (Bates, 1994; Thomas and Johnston, 1981). These agents can populate and perceive an open world environment like the one we inhabit (Laird and VanLent, 2001; Bates, 1994), and strive to behave in ways that exhibit emergent behaviors grounded in social interactions with users or other agents with the aim of becoming believable proxies of our behavior in hypothetical simulations of individuals and communities (McCoy et al., 2012; Burkinshaw, 2009; Francis, 2010). Historically, these agents were developed in the context of intelligent game non-player characters (NPCs) (Laird and VanLent, 2001; Riedl, 2012).
