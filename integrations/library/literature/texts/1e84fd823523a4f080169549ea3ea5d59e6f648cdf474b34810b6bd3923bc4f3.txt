The components above are, with few exceptions, evaluated in one setting and treated as static artifacts, and three gaps remain that we close:

1. Threat-type decidability. None of these works partition the threat space into a rule-decidable class and a rule-undecidable class and prove (via a determined steelman) that rules cannot cover the latter — the core conceptual move that justifies needing a judge in the first place rather than just asserting it (§2.1, Pillar 2). 2. Generalization across distribution and provider. Classifier guardrails such as Llama Guard, Prompt Guard, and DeBERTa detectors are themselves trained models and are known to degrade off-distribution and under simple evasions [Meta Prompt Guard; Dong et al. 2024]. We instead characterize, on a realistic held-out (out-of-distribution) corpus, that hand-authored rules collapse (48% accuracy) while a strong general judge nearly doubles them (84–85%) with near-zero false-blocks, and — critically — that this holds across two model providers and a capability ladder (recall saturates even at a small model; capability buys precision). This is Pillar 1, and it is a measurement/positioning result, not a new model. 3.
