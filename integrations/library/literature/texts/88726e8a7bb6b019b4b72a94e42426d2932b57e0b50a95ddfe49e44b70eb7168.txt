In order to analyze features, we construct "proxies" for each feature which can be easily computed. Our proxies are the log-likelihood ratio of a string under the feature hypothesis and under the full empirical distribution. For example, \log(P(s|\text{base64}) / P(s)) or \log(P(s|\text{Arabic Script}) / P(s)). We use log-likelihood proxies on the intuition that features, since they linearly interact with the logits, will be incentivized to track log-likelihoods. Since the activation of a feature at a token may be in part due to the preceding tokens (for example the Arabic feature could fire more strongly towards the end of a long Arabic string), we maximize the log-likelihood ratio over different prefixes leading up to and including the final token. But how can we estimate the different terms? Let's consider each part separately. Estimating P(s)

To compute P(s), we use the unigram distribution over tokens and compute P(s) = \prod_{t\in s} p(t). Estimating P(s|\text{base64}) (and other characters features)

For base64, we assume that we're modeling a random base64 string where the probability of a character c is P(c) = 1/64 if it's in a base64 character, and 10^{-10} otherwise.
