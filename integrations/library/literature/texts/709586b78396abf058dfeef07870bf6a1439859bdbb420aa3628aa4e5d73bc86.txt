In short, auditors should ideally not be selected or paid directly by auditees. Auditor tenure

The tenure of auditors is another key determinant of independence (Church et al., 2015). There is strong evidence that auditor-client familiarity can induce auditors to make judgments that are favorable to their client. One analysis of nearly 17,000 supplier audits found that supply chain monitors are more lenient when auditing entities they have inspected before (Short et al., 2016). Another study found that credit ratings teams were less accurate in their evaluations when they interacted directly with clients (Griffin and Tang, 2011). One approach to reduce concerns regarding familiarity is seen in financial auditing, where independence rules prohibit close relationships between auditors and their clients, and prevent the auditor from assisting in the preparation of the financial statements. If an auditor violates these independence rules, regulators can impose a range of consequences ranging from monetary sanctions to a bar from the industry (sec, 1995). Some have proposed mandatory audit rotations to counteract this problem.
