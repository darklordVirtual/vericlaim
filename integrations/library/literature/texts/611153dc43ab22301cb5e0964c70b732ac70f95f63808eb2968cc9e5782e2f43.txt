In these visions, computationally-powered agents act consistently with their past experiences and react believably to their environments. Such simulations of human behavior could populate virtual spaces and communities with realistic social phenomena (Dill and Martin, 2011; Park et al., 2022), train people on how to handle rare yet difficult interpersonal situations (Tambe et al., 1995; Jones et al., 1999; Hollan et al., 1984), test social science theories (Binz and Schulz, 2023; Horton, 2023), craft model human processors for theory and usability testing (Card et al., 1983; John and Kieras, 1996; Hämäläinen et al., 2023), power ubiquitous computing applications (Fast et al., 2016) and social robots (Bates, 1994; Bledsoe, 1986), and underpin non-playable game characters (Laird and VanLent, 2001; Riedl, 2012) that can navigate complex human relationships in an open world. However, the space of human behavior is vast and complex (Riedl, 2012; Yannakakis, 2012).
