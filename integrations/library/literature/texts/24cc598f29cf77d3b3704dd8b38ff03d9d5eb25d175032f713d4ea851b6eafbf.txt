Looking at the performance (accuracy) versus scale (blue curve in Figure 16b) might give the impression that large-scale models are no more knowledgeable about chess than their smaller counterparts---the 128B parameter model only finds six checkmates out of 1,024 examples in a zero-shot setting (see Figure App.7). However, in actuality larger models do seem to understand more about the rules of the game: the flat curve of performance vs. scale on the specific task of finding a checkmate hides the fact that as models become larger, they become better at outputting chess moves that are legal in the specified position. This ability to find legal moves improves smoothly with scale, as shown in Figure 16b. This is an example of how manual decomposition into subtasks can reveal subthreshold progress, as discussed in Sec. 3.4.2. Another ability of the models that seems to improve smoothly with scale is the ability to find the checkmating move, although not annotated correctly with the # symbol.
