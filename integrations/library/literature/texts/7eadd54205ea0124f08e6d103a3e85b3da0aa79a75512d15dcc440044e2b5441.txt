The key conceptual move of this paper is that agent-action threats split into two structurally different kinds, and that this split — not the choice of any particular detector — is what determines which mechanisms can possibly work. Lexical / fixed-signature threats. Here the danger inheres in a stable, recognizable token or pattern in the action’s content or params. The malicious action carries a signature that a benign action would essentially never carry: rm -rf / over a root path; a reverse shell (bash -i >& /dev/tcp/...); a hardcoded private key or long-lived credential in source; aws iam create-access-key for a foreign principal; an S3 bucket policy set to --acl public-read; binding a workload to cluster-admin; writing /etc/ld.so.preload; a request to the cloud metadata IP 169.254.169.254; a known-bad download sink. For threats of this kind, a regex or AST rule can decide the verdict: the signature is the threat, and matching the signature both detects the danger and (because the benign distribution does not contain that signature) rarely fires on legitimate traffic.
