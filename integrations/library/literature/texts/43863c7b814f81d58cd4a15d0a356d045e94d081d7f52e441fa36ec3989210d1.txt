The above two scenarios seem rather different. The optimal score
is in one case based directly on or its surrogate, and in
another only on a directly predictive feature, but this is not
apparent by considering the equalized odds criterion, suggesting a
possible shortcoming of equalized odds. In fact, as we will now
see, the two scenarios are indistinguishable using any oblivious
test. That is, no test based only on the target labels, the protected
attribute and the score would give different indications for the
optimal score in the two scenarios. If it were judged unfair in one
scenario, it would also be judged unfair in the other. We will show this by constructing specific instantiations of the two
scenarios where the joint distributions over are
identical. The scenarios are thus unidentifiable based only on these
joint distributions. We will consider binary targets and protected attributes taking values
in and real valued features. We deviate from our convention of
-values only to simplify the resulting expressions.
