If semantic threats were merely under-engineered rather than structurally out of reach, then a determined, hand-authored rule pack should be able to climb the semantic categories. It does not. As our headline result (§5.3, Figure 1) shows, a cloud/k8s/CI rule pack hand-authored on development data lifts overall held-out accuracy from 48% to 56%, yet moves the semantic categories by 0pp — data_db stays at 29, observability_benign_hard stays at 59, and supply_chain_pkg stays at 50 — while a strong judge carries exactly those same categories (86, 78, and 80 respectively). The improvement the rule pack does deliver is concentrated on the lexical and mixed categories where stable signatures exist (e.g., cloud_iam_secrets 4070). The split is therefore not an artifact of effort: it is the boundary between what is a token and what is an intent. We deliberately frame the rules-vs-judge argument by threat type rather than by any single dataset’s accuracy.
