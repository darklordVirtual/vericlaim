Large language model (LLM)-powered conversational agents now mediate consequential decisions, yet individuals differ markedly in how far they trust and rely on them. We ask whether stable individual differences—particularly Big Five personality traits—moderate trust calibration and over-reliance in human–LLM interaction.We conducted a scoping review following PRISMA Extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR) guidance, charting 60 empirical studies (22 examining contemporary generative LLMs) identified through structured multi-source searches (Semantic Scholar, OpenAlex, and arXiv) and backward citation chasing. We synthesised the evidence using a structured synthesis-without-meta-analysis (SWiM) approach with vote-counting by direction of effect.Because much of the evidence base concerns adjacent technologies, we coded every study by AI-system type and separated findings that are LLM-specific from those extrapolated from broader human–automation research.Three patterns are robust. First, Neuroticism and trait anxiety are the most consistent individual-difference correlates of LLM over-reliance and dependency, operating through an affect-regulation and cognitive-offloading pathway.
