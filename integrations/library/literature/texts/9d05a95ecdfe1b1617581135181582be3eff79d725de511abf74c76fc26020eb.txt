In implementation, however, these approaches often simplified the environment or dimensions of agent behavior to make the effort more manageable (Brooks et al., 2000; Minsky and Papert, 1970). Rule-based approaches, such as finite-state machines (Siu et al., 2021; Umarov et al., 2012) and behavior trees (Knafla, 2011; Pillosu, 2009; Hecker, 2011) account for the brute force approach of human-authoring the agent’s behavior (McCoy et al., 2012). They provide a straightforward way of creating simple agents that is still the most dominant approach today (McCoy et al., 2009; Miyashita et al., 2017; Yannakakis, 2012), and can even handle rudimentary social interactions, as shown in games such as Mass Effect (BioWare, 2007) and The Sims (Arts, 2009) series. Nonetheless, manually crafting behavior that can comprehensively address the breadth of possible interactions in an open world is untenable. This means that the resulting agent behaviors may not fully represent the consequences of their interactions (McCoy et al., 2012, 2011a, 2011b), and cannot perform new procedures that were not hard-coded in their script (Siu et al., 2021; Umarov et al., 2012).
