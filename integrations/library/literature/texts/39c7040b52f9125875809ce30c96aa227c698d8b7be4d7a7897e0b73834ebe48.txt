Language models (LMs)—statistical models which assign a probability to a sequence of words—are fundamental to many natural language processing tasks. Modern neural-network-based LMs use very large model architectures (e.g., 175 billion parameters [7]) and train on massive datasets (e.g., nearly a terabyte of English text [55]). This scaling increases the ability of LMs to generate fluent natural language [76, 53, 74], and also allows them to be applied to a plethora of other tasks [55, 29, 39], even without updating their parameters [7]. At the same time, machine learning models are notorious for exposing information about their (potentially private) training data—both in general [65, 47] and in the specific case of language models [8, 45].
