AI agents increasingly take consequential actions—shell commands, cloud operations, and arbitrary tool-calls—so a trust layer must decide, per action, whether to allow, warn, block, or escalate. We argue that the right way to reason about such a layer is by threat type. Lexical (fixed-signature) threats, where danger lives in a stable token, are decidable by deterministic rules; semantic (intent-dependent) threats, where a benign and a malicious action share the same surface, are out of reach for rules by construction. We make this concrete with a negative proof: a determined, hand-authored cloud rule pack lifts held-out accuracy only 4856% overall and moves the semantic categories by 0pp (data_db 2929, observability 5959, supply_chain 5050), while a strong LLM judge carries exactly those categories. We give the judge a self-learning capability—on a corpus that is mainly semantic attacks it nearly doubles rule accuracy (48% 83.6–85.2%) with near-zero false-blocks, and this holds across two model providers.
