In recent years, large language models have demonstrated impressive skills across many diverse tasks (Wang et al., 2019, Brown et al., 2020). Kaplan et al. (2020) describe the consistent benefits of increasing model size, characterizing scaling trends that hold across many orders of magnitude. However, even the largest models falter when required to perform multi-step mathematical reasoning (Hendrycks et al., 2021). Model samples frequently contain catastrophic mistakes, even after the model has been appropriately finetuned. Mathematical reasoning thus reveals a critical weakness in modern language models. One significant challenge in mathematical reasoning is the high sensitivity to individual mistakes (Shen et al., 2021a). When generating a solution, autoregressive models have no mechanism to correct their own errors. Solutions that veer off-course quickly become unrecoverable. If we rely purely on generative methods and extrapolate from current trends, we will require an exorbitant parameter count to achieve even moderate performance on distributions as challenging as the MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021).
